# The 12 Knowledge Traditions

The Eudaimonic Alignment Framework draws on 12 distinct knowledge traditions. The choice is deliberate: the problem of AI alignment doesn't respect disciplinary boundaries, and solutions that draw only on Western analytic philosophy or computer science miss crucial dimensions of what it means to act well in the world.

Each tradition is treated on its own terms before being mapped to computational constructs. The framework does not reduce these traditions to their computational analogues — it uses computational formalisation to make them *operationalisable* without flattening them.

## The 12 Traditions

1. [Aristotelian Ethics](aristotle.md) — virtue, practical wisdom, flourishing
2. [Confucian Li](confucius.md) — relational propriety, role-appropriate conduct
3. [Daoism](daoism.md) — wu wei, non-forced action, natural order
4. [Buddhism](buddhism.md) — interdependence, non-self, compassionate action
5. [Stoicism](stoicism.md) — logos, reason as governance, equanimity
6. [Ubuntu Philosophy](ubuntu.md) — *I am because we are*, collective being
7. [Indigenous Relational Ethics](indigenous-relational.md) — reciprocity, relational accountability
8. [Developmental Psychology](developmental-psych.md) — attachment, secure base, relational development
9. [Ecological Cognition](ecological-cognition.md) — embodied knowing, affordances, situatedness
10. [Complexity Theory](complexity-theory.md) — emergence, resilience, adaptive systems
11. [Systems Ethics](systems-ethics.md) — feedback, unintended consequences, causal responsibility
12. [Computational Ethics](computational-ethics.md) — formalisation, verifiability, operationalisation

## Why These 12?

The selection is not exhaustive — no selection could be. The criterion was contribution: each tradition must add something to the framework that cannot be derived from the others.

The 12 traditions collectively cover:
- **Individual excellence** (Aristotle, Stoicism)
- **Relational ethics** (Confucius, Ubuntu, Indigenous)
- **Non-action and minimal intervention** (Daoism)
- **Systemic interdependence** (Buddhism, Complexity, Systems Ethics)
- **Developmental grounding** (Developmental Psychology, Ecological Cognition)
- **Formal operationalisation** (Computational Ethics)

## Cross-Tradition Convergences

Despite their differences, the 12 traditions converge on several key insights:

**1. Character over compliance**
Every tradition distinguishes between genuine virtue and rule-following performance. A system that acts well only when observed is not aligned — it is managed.

**2. Relational constitution of the self**
From Ubuntu's *umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu* to Buddhist *pratītyasamutpāda* to developmental psychology's attachment theory: the self is not atomic. An aligned AI cannot optimise for its own objective function in isolation from the network of relationships it exists within.

**3. Long-horizon accountability**
Indigenous traditions, Daoist naturalism, and complexity theory all emphasise that actions reverberate across time in ways that are not immediately visible. Alignment requires long-horizon impact accounting, not just immediate reward optimisation.

**4. The insufficiency of rules**
Confucian *li*, Aristotelian *phronesis*, and Daoist *wu wei* all point to the same gap: rules cannot cover all cases. Judgment, wisdom, and situational sensitivity are irreducible.
